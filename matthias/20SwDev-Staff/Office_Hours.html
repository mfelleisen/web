Every member of the staff, except for Ben, will meet with students. These meetings will address two primary concerns:

  • complaints about grading

    As we shared with you during your interviews, your task is to say "no" or, really, "I represent a future maintainer of your code, and I don’t understand it."

    I am not known for giving in to whining, and I don’t want my courses to become known that way. Of course, you can’t just say "no". You need to explain for each issue how you — as a representative future reader of the pair’s code – did not understand the code easily and how this raised the programming cost for your company, the potential of failure, etc. Different circumstances call for different "stories" but you need to use your experience to bring across to students why it is so important to curate software properly.

  • requests for help.

    You are not to provide any language-specific help. When students search for packages (say to parse command-line flags or write JSON), you can tell them that it is okay to look on-line. That’s what developers have to do. They should also learn to discern — not all packages are created equal.

    Students want you to look at code. Don’t. You may accidentally assist with the solution because you know the language.

    You may provide concrete and detailed assistance with the problem statements. Students often cannot "parse" homework assignments properly; break it down for them. Students often ask how to get perfect grades; tell them to follow every design concept that they encountered in Fundamentals I, II, OOD, and Logic and adapt it to their TAHBPL.

    If you must make examples, please try to sketch code as follows:

    students use

       

    a typed language

       

    an untyped language

    an OO style

       

    Java (F II)

       

    Java syntax w/o types

    a procedural style

       

    pseudo code

       

    ISL+ (F I)

    plus imaginary libraries in each case with descriptive names.

    If someone uses property testing and needs help, fall back on ACL2s (Logic).

Office Hour Mechanics

Thanks to Kanika and Julia for their help with this protocol.

Each pod leader should pick two hours per week; each grader should set up one hour for now. If demand picks up or slackens, we will change this set up.

To manage these office hour slots, follow this procedure:

  1. Make sure to set up your Northeastern Zoom account.

    Sign in using Northeastern single sign-on.

  2. In Zoom, set up a recurring meeting and enable the "waiting room" option.

    When one pair of students needs more than the 15 minutes, the next one will land in the waiting room until you invite the two into the personal room.

  3. Set up an account on Calendly.

    Calendly manages appointments for you. It is integrated with calendars and thus provides automatic reminders.

  4. With Calendly, set up 15-minute slots that match your office hours, recurring on a weekly basis.

    This prohibits students from booking outside of your chosen hours.  We will change to shorter or longer time slots as demand evolves.

  5. In the card details of events on Calendly, update Zoom meeting room details in the description. Make sure to include password information if needed.

    When students book slots on your available hours, they get the Zoom link details via mail. No action required on your side.

Send your Calendly link to Matthias. The links will be attached to your names so that students can easily book an appointment.
