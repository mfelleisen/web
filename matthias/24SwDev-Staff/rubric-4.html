Programming Inspection – everyoneðŸ”—

Check the player’s strategy functionality.

inspection: 130 points

– 20 points, for helpful self-eval

  • 10 points for a data definition that represents pebble exchanges;

  • 10 points for a data definition that represents the purchases of cards;

  • 5 points for the signature and purpose statement of a function/method that answers question 1;

  • 5 points for the signature and purpose statement of a helper function/method that determines which pebble exchanges a player wishes to conduct;

  • 10 points for the signature and purpose statement of a function/method that answers question 2; this may need an explanation because the functionality may be embedded in a complex method.

  • 5 points for the signature and purpose statement of a function/method for tie-breaking card purchases;

  • 5 points for the signature and purpose statement of a function/method for tie-breaking pebble trading;

  • 20 points for properly organizing the strategy component.

    The specification includes two strategies: one that maximizes points and one that maximizes the number of cards bought:

    • a functional design realizes the two strategy functions via higher-order function, that is, by passing in the difference as a function because it is a field access.

    • an OO design could use one of two possibilities:

      • early Fundamentals II design lessons teach that this situation calls for an interface, one abstract class, and two concrete classes. Technically this is called “template and hook” design. The concrete classes would contain “one line” methods for extracting the score of a series of card purchases or for counting the number of cards in a purchase.

      • late Fundamentals II/acc or Fundamentals III teaches the use of the command pattern. This would mimic the functional design and create rather concise solutions. The existence of “lambda” in Java makes this variant particularly appealing.

      • I am sure students will come up with alternative solutions. Read their justification and see how well the code realizes them.

    If their design fails to come with proper abstraction, subtract 20 points. A particularly bad way of abstracting is to pass in something like a Boolean that indicates whether one or the other maximization is desired. Subtract all points of you find such a solution, regardless of the justification.

    If it abstracts but fails to abstract all commonalities, subtract 10 points.

    For other mistakes concerning abstraction, go for a subtraction of 5 points.

  • 40 points for a unit test that shows, respectively,

    • no cards can be bought with the available pebbles

    • a card can be bought without exchanges of pebbles

    • a card can be bought after an exchange of pebbles

    • two cards can be bought after an exchange of pebbles

    10 each, no partial credit

There is no need to check whether these pieces work properly. Next week’s integration tests will do so.
