Matthias Felleisen is currently a Trustee Professor at Northeastern University. He joined its College of Computer and Information Science in 2001, after a 14-year career at Rice University in Houston with long and short sabbaticals at Carnegie Mellon University in Pittsburgh and École Normale Supérieure in Paris. He received his PhD from Daniel P. Friedman in 1987.

Felleisen's research career consists of two distinct periods. For the first, he focused on the semantics of programming languages and its applications. His work on operational semantics has become one of the standard working methods in programming languages. For the second, Felleisen and his research group (PLT) developed a novel introductory curriculum plus support software to run a nation-wide outreach K12 project. The curriculum uses methods from functional programming to teach systematic design and problem solving. The work on the support software has produced a novel functional programming language, called Racket, and an IDE (DrRacket) that can be tailored to the curriculum. For Felleisen and his team, the construction of a large, realistic software application has posed many interesting and challenging research problems in programming languages, component programming, software contracts, and software engineering.

Over the past 20 years, Felleisen has published close to 100 research papers in scientific journals, conferences, and magazines. In addition, he has co-authored six books, including How to Design Programs; The Little LISPer (now called The Little Schemer), which, at the age of 40, is one of the oldest continuously published books in the field; and Semantics Engineering, his most recent book on techniques for developing and maintaining the semantics of a programming language. In 2013, he also published Realm of Racket with eight undergraduate co-authors, graduates of his Program by Design course.

For his research on programming languages, Felleisen was inducted as an ACM Fellow in 2006 and received ACM SIGPLAN's Lifetime Achievement Award in 2012. Also in 2012, his paper on software contracts was picked as the most influential ICFP 2002 publication. For his educational outreach, ACM honored Felleisen with the Karl V. Karlstrom Award in 2010. ACM SIGCSE named Felleisen Outstanding Educator of the Year in 2011. Felleisen has delivered keynote addresses at many major programming language conferences, including POPL, ECOOP, ICFP as well as the Symposium on Computer Science Education.
