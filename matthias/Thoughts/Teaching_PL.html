Teaching PL

Matthias Felleisen

15 Apr 2020

Changed in version 1.0: Thu Apr 16 11:10:29 EDT 2020, first release

This spring semester I taught the undergraduate programming language course—titled “Principles of Programming Languages” (PPL) —for the first time in 18 years. Before that I had taught this course for 14 years in a row, with one break for a sabbatical.

Since 2002, I have continued to develop an undergraduate core curriculum that emphasizes the training of developers. My take is that 99% of our undergraduate students wish to have the chance to become a developer, and it is our job to give them the best possible preparation. I am reasonably satisfied with the progress we have made. (Once we give them the chance to work for top-tier software companies, it is up to them to say “no” and become doll makers. Or whatever.)

1 Why Do We Teach PL

I consider the programming languages course a building block in this “prepare them for a job” curriculum. But I understand that people teach an undergraduate course on programming languages for many different reasons.

Some instructors want to show students what their favorite language can do for them. Others wish to expose their class to ideas that are of exciting to them. Some hold a near-religious belief about the landscape of programming languages and how people must view it. If you belong to one of these groups, this note is not for you.

By contrast I expect this course to play two roles:

  • It is primarily an introduction to the fundamental ideas of programming languages that every developer must understand, those that end up working in C#, JavaScript, Haskell, Rust, or some other language.

  • It also reinforce the lessons of our core curriculum.

    The researchers in programming languages—the oldest area in computer science—have developed the deepest understanding of the development of programs. This understanding deeply informs the design of our curriculum. The assignments in this course will reinforce these lessons and demonstrate their usefulness.

2 Transfer

A problem that I have had with the way I taught PPL in the first 14 years of my career is that I never knew whether students could transfer the concepts from class to the languages they wanted to—or had to—use.

Several conversations with Shriram on students’ understanding of aliasing only reinforced this troubling thought.

3 My Approach

So, I decided to run my course like this:

  • let students work in their “favorite” language

  • use Racket in lectures as my “second favorite” language

  • explicitly work with abstract syntax (structs, not S-expressions; strings rarely symbols) only after dispatching with parsing as a “model view control” problem

  • prefer the functional style but occasionally give direct or indirect reminders on how this would work in an object-oriented language.

Not surprisingly, a large number of students chose Java the language of our second and third course, which many had just completed. An equally large number opted for Python of course. Otherwise there were a handful of (Typed) Racket pairs, and one each of Haskell, Rust, and TypeScript. One pair started in OCaml and, after reading the first or second homework assignment, switched to Typed Racket.

The concrete input syntax was JSON, though I mostly used nested arrays. Imagine S-expressions with strings instead of symbols and commas everywhere. — To drive home the idea of abstract syntax, one homework assignment pointed them to a web page with S-expression parsers for every one of the chosen languages and asked to parse two different concrete syntax choices into the same abstract syntax.

I addressed the “transfer problem” head-on with an assignment that asked to write a “transpiler” that translates the JSON input programs for their interpreters to programs in their chosen language. They had to measure for how many of around 100 tests their interpreter and their transpiled programs produced the same outputs. Below I refer to this as Homework 8 (HW8).

I specifically worked on aliasing with three different homework assignment that forced them to work through the idea at all levels: test programs, their own programming language, and the environment-store distinction. The good students clearly grokked it—and they may have without this re-design. But near the end of the semester, I started to identify a problem among the weak part of my class:

their questions suggested that they still had a hard time separating static scope/environment from dynamic effect/store and dynamic extent/continuation.

A conversation with Ben Lerner, who taught compilers in parallel with few students in both classes, reinforced this impression. Perhaps it’s just really really hard.

4 Student Response

I wanted to explore several aspects with Piazza polls once the pandemic hit but in the end I left it at three. As always, only about half the students voted though Piazza says that almost all of them looked at the post. I wonder what non-voting means here.

Figure 1: In which language do students wish to program?

The first question that interested me concerned the choice of programming language. As you can tell from figure 1 students supported this option with an overwhelming majority. But, to my surprise, a fair number voted for an option that I added as a teaser—have the instructor pick a number of languages. Only one (1!) person voted for letting the instructor choose.

Figure 2: What kind of input language should the course use?

The second question was about the input language. Frankly, I had hoped that the exercise on S-expression had convinced them of the cleanliness of S-expression over JSON. Well, figure 2 tells you that they love what they (think they) know.

Figure 3: What helps with the transfer of knowledge?

For the third question, I allowed students to check multiple options. The goal was to find out whether explicitly talking about transfer and explicitly assigning a “transfer homework” had helped. I think figure 3 leaves now doubt about how students feel.

4.1 Feel

I used the word “feel” here because I am perfectly aware that a Piazza poll is not scientific evidence. It does provide us with an impression of how students perceive the course and possibly suggests that explicitly addressing their hearts and minds helps them learn. And that may help us teach.

5 Postscript: Call/CC

I could not stomach asking my teaching assistants to grade cps-ed Java programs. So instead I pursued the following path.

First, I asked the students to implement a cps transpiler for our model language. I really like the way I taught cps-ing this year.

Second, for the last few weeks I switched from interpreters to C, CC, CEK, and CESK machines. I wanted to figure out how that went over but due to the pandemic they worked through just two homework assignments with abstract machines. And frankly, I didn’t know how to ask them about the abstract machine topic.

Acknowledgments Conversations with Shriram on his PL course reinforced my desire to try this approach. “Coffees” with my current PhD students plus Jason Heman and Ben Lerner served as a sounding board during the entire fall 2019 semester. Their comments helped me shape several elements; Michael Ballantyne’s encouragement to switch to abstract machines at the end helped me make the move that I had imagined ever since I wrote the Redex book with Matthew and Robby.
