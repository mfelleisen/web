Grading Process

All projects, rubrics, and self-evaluations will be available in the course repo by the first day of class. The section heads have access to the repo.

On the day the assignment is due, Ben will push a self-evaluation sheet into each repo. The pair has 24 hours to push a filled-in sheet back. They will get points for turning it in on time and for accuracy; the goal is to get them to reflect on code and view it from the perspective of a future reader/maintainer.

Your task is to simulate this future reader for them. For basically each code-inspection issue, they are expected to supply URLs to corresponding line ranges in github files. Hopefully this will also simplify your task and make it possible to complete the grading within five days of the due date for all project milestones.

Once homework solutions are assigned to graders, the heads will meet with their team and jointly sample-grade the solutions assigned to the head. The goal is for the team to develop a common interpretation of the rubric. The section heads will communicate with each other and MF to resolve ambiguities.

Graders should bring up any difficulties with their section heads or team first, depending on the concern (interpreting the rubric, grading a specific case, meeting with a pair, or being late with grading).

Once the graders have evaluated the students’ work, the section heads will go check a selection from each grader to enforce a reasonable degree of consistency.

Office Hours

Every member of the staff, except for Ben, will meet with students. These meetings will address two primary concerns:

  • complaints about grading

    As we shared with you during your interviews, your task is to say "no". I am not known for giving in to whining, and I don’t want my courses to become known that way. Of course, you can’t just say "no". You need to explain for each issue how you — as a representative future reader of the pair’s code – did not understand the code easily and how this raised the programming cost for your company, the potential of failure, etc. Different circumstances call for different "stories" but you need to use your experience to bring across to students why it is so important to curate software properly.

  • requests for help.

    You are not to provide any language-specific help. When students search for packages (say to parse command-line flags or write JSON), you can tell them that it is okay to look on-line. That’s what developers have to do. They should also learn to discern — not all packages are created equal.

    Students want you to look at code. Don’t. You may accidentally assist with the solution because you know the language.

    You may provide concrete and detailed assistance with the problem statements. Students often cannot "parse" homework assignments properly; break it down for them. Students often ask how to get perfect grades; tell them to follow every design concept that they encountered in Fundamentals I, II, OOD, and Logic and adapt it to their TAHBPL.
